# Bright Side of Inequality

It would be irresponsible to say that inequality is bad (which is argued in the following chapters) without asking what's good about it.
What is surprising is what the answer suggests: a recipe for growth (that we will also expand in a later chapter). 
Here we explore some of the many reasons why inequality might be good. 

## Incentives 
If everyone gets the same resources no matter what, people are not incentivized to do anything different.
In contrast, if doing more gets you more, or if doing something different gets you more, people are incentivized to do more or to do something different.
Inequality on income then allows for performance-based compensation and moves workers to the jobs that pay more, which are those who argueably need them more. Inequality allows for competition among workers as well, which pushes them to make efforts in their jobs and to develop valuable skills. 

Also, inequality in the rewards to entrepreneurship or investment incentivizes risk-taking. This leads to more people trying, more research and development, and riskier projects being considered. All this leads to higher productivity and growth.

Even when choosing what to study people look at the job market. Assigning more 
workers to the most needed positions also contributes to higher productivity and growth. 

## Innovation

Inequality can also spur innovation, not only through its consequences on the incentives, but also because richer people create markets that make new goods and services financially viable. Most products we have now, such as phones or bicycles, were once luxury items. Without very rich people the low-scale high-cost initial stage of most products is harder to justify financially, which lowers innovation and therefore growth.


## Financial Implications 

One interesting thing about some people having more resources than other people (a.k.a. inequality) is that they can use these resources to invest in their own risky projects without relying on credit. And if it goes well, they would have even more resources to invest in another risky project. So there is a sort of self-selection and self-financing going on that is very good when credit markets are not very developed (they are known to be very risk-averse).

Also, some people having a lot of capital gives room for very big or hard-to-finance projects to be carried out. Inequality allows a rich person obsessed with space to create a rocket company. Pyramids are amazing cultural heritage, but we wouldn't have them had Egypt been more egalitarian economically.

## Public Good 

Inequality allows also for elite-funded public goods and philanthropy, which can address things that the states don't. For instance, the Gates foundation has reportedly done great things for global health, such as fighting polio or malaria, which the world was not taking care of.


## Conclusion

Inequality has prenty of good things, such as the incentives it provides or the diversity of markets it creates. Also, inequality can compensate the mistakes of most credit systems and enable riskier investments. It also enables more powerful philanthropy, which can many times do better than the states by many measures.
