# Introduction

I hate to write about this. I wanted to write a beautiful book on economics, explaining the issues and the solutions. Because the economy is vital. But I'm here writing about something else. Something I thought was less important. I don't like Acemoglu, he said China was going to fail because it's not a democracy, and he didn't really develop a predictive model or a recipe on how to make good institutions. But he was right on the importance of institutions, and this is very boring. But we need to speak about this, it's urgent, probably because it's so dull that few people consider it.

What's "this"? We can discuss long about what to do. I had beautiful ideas. But who cares? We know what to do: if climate change is really an issue and the cause is burning fuel, we need to stop burning fuel. If inequality is really causing problems, we need to reduce inequality. If inmigration is causing issues, we need to stop inmigration. Of course, the solutions might be hard to design, but humans are really good at that. You pay some experts to work on the solution, they will surely come up with something reasonable, and then you implement it. Carbon tariffs and a push in renewable/nuclear energy. Non-evadable wealth taxes. Functional border control and the legal and institutional framework to deal with legal and illegal inmigrants. The solutions are out there. But the political issues are huge. There's a reason why pension systems are broken in most of the world and they're not fixed, there's a reason why the US is drowning into debt and the politicians won't rescue their own country. The reason is that the system doesn't work.

Let me be clear. We shouldn't be poor. We are somewhat poor because many societies don't have broadly distributed productivity, which makes the life of many people hard. And all the issues I mentioned above are issues that have been noticed decades ago. Yet no-one did what was needed. This is because power isn't in the hands of capable good-intentioned people. Even when some of the power is in their hands, these well intentioned people do not deliberate with other capable well intentioned people with different views (yes, not all capable well-intentioned people have the same views). And even if they deliberated, making things happen is hard. People have gotten used to "this works badly, tough luck". If society doesn't work well, something should be fixed. But we are missing the mechanisms to make society better.

Democracy has nice features. It accepts pluralism, i.e., it accepts that people might have opposed opinions and be right (e.g., freedom vs. harmony), and says "we might think differently but we live together and need to act together". Democracy provides a colliseum where people fight for the power to define the direction of the collective. That fight is framed in a certain way which supposedly makes it productive. 

Hierarchies also have nice features. The Chinese run the state similarly to how the US runs companies. You have a board and a CEO, and most of the times they agree. Things are pretty meritocratic in both cases. This allows for stability and strategic long-term planning.

And the systems matter a lot. Not only it determines who gets to power, but also how decisions are made, and whether feedback is used or ignored. All the problems we have are the result of suboptimal decision making in the past. We're missing out on a great future by continuing to make bad choices. And no-one seems to think about why we make bad choices. We don't need to discuss WHAT to do, we need to discuss HOW do we find the best actions we agree on. Let's explore that.

# Big picture

In a nutshell, the most contested thing are values. The process for defining the values should be controlled, but we shouldn't expect it to be collaborative, it will be most likely violent (power fight). Violence here means liquid democracy (defined later). Once the values are defined, we can use them to evaluate everything. 

Then we build a to-do list, and sort it according to our values. The most impactful items will appear clearly, and these should receive more attention. But many things could be tackled in parallel if there is capacity. Responsabilities involve 1. providing the possible action items and 2. acting on them. 

Another important piece is feedback. Can anything be improved? Add it as an action item. Feedback should be collected automatically, easily created, and always considered.

Last but not least, a deliberation platform should be created. This involves anonymization of commenters and automated fallacy detection. This is similar to tools inside Bridgewater's idea meritocracy. 


# Values 

Pluralism is about values. Reasonable people usually agree. But sometimes things get emotional. Abortion is an example. You can reason all you want about this, but if a person thinks that the law should forbid homicide, and thinks abortion is homicide, while the other person thinks the law should reduce the number of deaths and doesn't count the fetus as a death, then they won't agree. Even though it would be nice for people to understand that their values are no better than others', it rarely happens and we judge and we fight to impose our positions on other people. The first step for good decision making is to define common values collectively, which involves some fighting.

It would be nice to agree on "we value freedom more than equality". But what does that mean? Even "human rights" are highly contested and full with nuances. Values should be questions. They should be the questions we ask to evaluate each idea. They might be comparative questions, e.g., "is A more _value_ than B?", or direct, "how much _value_ is A?".

Tools help us structure our thoughts. The most important tool is probably the sorted list. This is a list of items (actions, problems, values) where the most important go first. The most important thing to do is to define what are the most important things to do. 

# Critics

You ignore incentives—no system lasts if it doesn’t align personal and collective rewards.

You treat “values” as stable; they shift with context and power.

The feedback loop needs enforcement, not just collection. Who acts on it?

Collective intelligence collapses without trust calibration—how do we know whose opinion to weight?

Expertise and legitimacy conflict; your design needs a bridge between technocracy and democracy.

Power rotation sounds good but kills continuity; address institutional memory.

Sorting “what matters most” assumes we can measure value—how?

Deliberation platforms tend to amplify noise unless attention itself is rationed.

You need failure protocols: how to reverse bad collective decisions without paralysis.

Ethics of manipulation: if you’re designing systems that shape decisions, who guards against engineered consent?
