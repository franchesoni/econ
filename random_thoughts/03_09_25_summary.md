So people discuss land value tax, and indeed it's interesting. Then people discuss wealth tax. And it is interesting indeed. Wealth in fact is a combination between money-generating assets and static assets. For instance, gold is static. You could, as a rich person, buy gold and sit on it, and maybe you'll see your money grow. If you need, you can sell a little of it. That's an interesting thing though. The very rich are allocated a lot of resources but they do not necessarily consume them. They do buy luxury stuff, but many just pass it down to their kids or so. They sometimes do spend the things in investments. For instance Elon in a way reinvests all of his money, and his consumption is not too high. But he can pay people to do almost anything he wants, which is kind of mind-boggling. He can, for instance, try to do politics. And if he fails, it's not because of lack of money, but because of lack of character and because he's not the only one with power playing the game.

So it's very interesting to think about real resources, who owns them, and what happens. When the rich have power, most of the real resources are built for the rich, instead of for the public. So resources are put into the creation of space ships for space tourism, yatches, etc. I like this citation 'Advertising is probably the world champ of “incredible resource use, almost no real social return.” It eats the smartest designers, psychologists, data scientists, entire AI clusters… all to convince someone to buy sneakers they didn’t want.'

So in short fixing the economy is about making real resources go where they are most needed. And that is the working class. However, one should be cautious. In the kibuts (i don't know what thye were called) many people left because they were young and they wanted something better. That's tricky. If you're good in place A, but place A doesn't allow for much inequality, then you are better off moving to place B, where you will get more. This is, in itself, a solution to the problem. This "being good" means in fact "generating value" or "being a good human resource". We don't need to penalize (more than other countries) the people that make a lot of money by working. We simply need to penalize people that get money simply by owning assets. Now the issue here is that owning assets might be done actively or passively. The ownership of the asset could be seen as a kind of pay, it's a big motivation driver, and "goodwill" is the blood of entrepreneurship. So if the person that is good in place A, is good in terms of company building, but they know that they will keep much more of the company if moving to place B, we might lose them and that's bad. It remains to be seen how important these exceptional people are. I think that would be a big discussion. Now the issue is that even if the person creates the company in place B, when selling in place A, the person needs to pay taxes. Not the person itself but the company. Again, we talk about companies, but we should speak about resources. And companies are the stuff-generating machines of our economies. The only thing keeping a conutry from implementing a nice tax reform is other countries. What happens if the people go away? How can you tax companies that sell exports to your country? I mean designing taxation (as a way to devote correctly real resources) is way easier in a closed system, so one thing should be about designing this in a closed system, this is, international cooperation. The second thing would be to solve the local system with the border constrains. THat's a nice solution, first solve it closed, then solve it with border constrains. Implement both.   

In the end the economy is simple, it's about incentives (which conditions production) and distribution. And incentives and distribution are the same. So it's all about distribution. Who gets what, when and why.
